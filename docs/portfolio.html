Austin, TX — A Demographic Portrait

Introduction

Austin-Round Rock is one of the fastest-growing metropolitan areas in the United States. Driven by domestic in-migration, a booming tech sector, and significant Sunbelt population redistribution, the region has undergone dramatic demographic and economic transformation over the past two decades. This portfolio uses five years of American Community Survey (ACS) data and a suite of spatial analysis methods to examine who lives in Austin, where they live, and how housing costs, education, and race intersect across the metro’s five-county extent — Travis, Williamson, Hays, Bastrop, and Caldwell.

Collin County, a wealthy suburban county in the Dallas-Fort Worth metro, leads Texas with over 52% of adults holding a bachelor’s degree, while rural Loving County anchors the bottom with a statistically unreliable 0% estimate due to its fewer than 100 residents. The statewide median of 17.3% reflects the wide gap between highly educated urban counties and rural areas with lower attainment. Travis County, home to Austin, sits well above this median, consistent with the region’s concentration of university graduates and tech workers.

The margin of error plot reveals substantial income inequality across Texas’s 254 counties. Rockwall County leads with the highest estimated median household income, while several rural counties in South and West Texas fall near or below $30,000. Notably, the Austin metro counties, Travis, Williamson, and Hays, cluster in the upper-middle range, consistent with the region’s high-wage tech economy. Wide error bars on lower-income counties reflect small sample sizes in sparsely populated rural areas, a direct limitation of ACS estimation.

Austin’s population pyramid tells the story of a city that people are actively choosing to move to. The pronounced bulge in the 25–34 age bands reflects an influx of recent graduates and tech workers drawn by the city’s growing economy. A solid base of children under 14 suggests that many of these young adults are settling down and starting families. Notably thin older age bands distinguish Austin from retirement-heavy metros like Phoenix or Miami. Overall the pyramid resembles a diamond, narrow at the top and bottom, widest through the working-age middle.

Part 2: Where Do People Live? — Spatial Patterns in Housing

Rent Hotspot Analysis

Show code 
library(sf)
library(spdep)
library(tmap)
library(tigris)

options(tigris_use_cache = TRUE)

austin_rent <- get_acs(
  geography = "tract",
  variables = c(rent = "B25064_001"),
  state = "TX",
  county = c("Travis", "Williamson", "Hays", "Bastrop", "Caldwell"),
  year = 2020,
  geometry = TRUE
) %>%
  st_transform(6580) %>%
  filter(!is.na(estimate))

neighbors <- poly2nb(austin_rent, queen = TRUE)
weights <- nb2listw(neighbors, style = "W", zero.policy = TRUE)

gi_values <- localG(austin_rent$estimate,
                    listw = weights,
                    zero.policy = TRUE)

austin_rent <- austin_rent %>%
  mutate(
    gi_star = as.numeric(gi_values),
    cluster = case_when(
      gi_star >= 2.576 ~ "High Cluster (99%)",
      gi_star >= 1.960 ~ "High Cluster (95%)",
      gi_star >= 1.645 ~ "High Cluster (90%)",
      gi_star <= -2.576 ~ "Low Cluster (99%)",
      gi_star <= -1.960 ~ "Low Cluster (95%)",
      gi_star <= -1.645 ~ "Low Cluster (90%)",
      TRUE ~ "Not significant"
    )
  )

tmap_mode("plot")

tm_shape(austin_rent) +
  tm_polygons(
    col = "cluster",
    palette = c(
      "High Cluster (99%)" = "#b2182b",
      "High Cluster (95%)" = "#ef8a62",
      "High Cluster (90%)" = "#fddbc7",
      "Low Cluster (90%)"  = "#d1e5f0",
      "Low Cluster (95%)"  = "#67a9cf",
      "Low Cluster (99%)"  = "#2166ac",
      "Not significant"    = "grey85"
    ),
    title = "Gi* Cluster Type"
  ) +
  tm_layout(
    main.title = "Median Gross Rent Hotspots\nAustin Metro, 2016–2020 ACS",
    frame = FALSE,
    legend.outside = TRUE
  )

The Getis-Ord Gi* hotspot analysis reveals a clear spatial pattern in rental costs across the Austin metro. High-rent clusters at the 99% confidence level concentrate in central Austin and the northwest corridor, particularly around the Domain area, a major tech employment hub home to Apple and Amazon whose workers drive demand for high-end nearby housing. Emerging mid-level clusters pushing eastward from downtown reflect ongoing gentrification of historically lower-income east Austin neighborhoods. Low-rent clusters in the outer suburban and rural fringe of Bastrop and Caldwell counties reflect the cost advantages of distance from major employment centers.

The local multi-group segregation map reveals pronounced spatial clustering, with the highest scores concentrated in tracts of eastern Austin and isolated pockets in the southern suburbs. These high-segregation tracts correspond closely to historically Hispanic and Black neighborhoods east of I-35, a highway that has functioned as a de facto racial dividing line since the 1960s. The western and northwestern portions of the metro show consistently low segregation scores, reflecting the predominantly non-Hispanic White and relatively homogeneous character of those neighborhoods.

library(rmapshaper)

tx_tracts <- get_acs(
  geography = "tract",
  year = 2023,
  variables = c(
    tpop  = "B03002_001",

    black = "B03002_004",

  state = "TX",

  ) %>%
  rename(tpop = tpopE, white = whiteE,
         asian = asianE, black = blackE, hisp = hispE) %>%

pl <- places(state = "TX", year = 2023, cb = TRUE)

austin_tracts <- ms_clip(

  select(tpop, pwhite, phisp, white, GEOID.x,

The same eastern corridor that drives high local segregation scores is precisely where Hispanic concentration is highest.

Part 4: Why? — Modeling the Patterns

Geographically Weighted Regression

The GWR local R-squared map shows that the model predictors explain home value variation well across most of the metro, with the strongest fit concentrated in Williamson County and the southeastern suburbs where development patterns are relatively uniform. The weakest fit appears in the urban core of Travis County and the northwestern exurban fringe, where local amenity premiums and the legacy of historic disinvestment east of I-35 drive home values in ways not fully captured by the global predictors. The college education coefficient map reveals that educational attainment has the strongest effect on home values in southern Austin, where variation in attainment between nearby neighborhoods is greatest, and a weaker effect in uniformly high-education suburbs like Round Rock and Georgetown.

Geodemographic Clustering

Show code 
set.seed(1983)

austin_estimates <- austin_data_for_model %>%
  select(-GEOID, -median_value, -median_year_built) %>%
  st_drop_geometry()

pca_austin <- prcomp(
  formula = ~.,
  data = austin_estimates,
  scale. = TRUE,
  center = TRUE
)

components_austin <- predict(pca_austin, austin_estimates)

austin_pca <- austin_data_for_model %>%
  select(GEOID, median_value) %>%
  cbind(components_austin)

austin_kmeans <- austin_pca %>%
  st_drop_geometry() %>%
  select(PC1:PC8) %>%
  kmeans(centers = 6)

austin_clusters <- austin_pca %>%
  mutate(cluster = as.character(austin_kmeans$cluster))

ggplot(austin_clusters, aes(fill = cluster)) +
  geom_sf(size = 0.1, color = "white") +
  scale_fill_brewer(palette = "Dark2") +
  theme_void() +
  labs(
    fill = "Cluster",
    title = "Geodemographic Clusters — Austin Metro Area",
    subtitle = "K-means clustering on ACS variables, 2016–2020",
    caption = "Source: ACS; tidycensus R package"
  )

The K-means cluster map identifies six distinct neighborhood typologies across the Austin metro. The outer exurban fringe of all five counties is dominated by lower-density rural and semi-rural communities, while eastern Travis County and the southeastern metro show a cluster consistent with lower-income, majority-minority neighborhoods. The dense urban core shows the greatest cluster diversity, aligning with the GWR result that model fit is weakest where local complexity is highest.

Conclusion

Across five labs and four methods, exploratory data analysis, spatial clustering, segregation statistics, and geographically weighted regression, a coherent story emerges about Austin. It is a young, highly educated, economically dynamic metro that has attracted enormous in-migration. But that growth has come with costs. Housing prices have risen sharply, concentrated in the tech-adjacent northwest and gentrifying east side. Racial residential segregation, anchored by the I-35 divide that dates to the 1960s, remains a defining feature of the city’s spatial structure. And the outer suburban fringe, while offering lower rents, sits far from the employment centers driving the region’s growth. Austin’s trajectory raises fundamental questions about who benefits from urban economic success, and who gets pushed to its edges. —
